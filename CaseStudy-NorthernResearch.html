High Arctic Research Off-Grid

Autonomous Water for High Arctic Research: Engineering AWG-RO Survival at −45°C

AR
Aarvish Engineering & Field Operations Team
Deployment Station: Resolute Bay Climate Observatory · Nunavut, Canada · 74.7°N
Executive Summary

Between September 2024 and December 2025, Aarvish deployed a hardened AWG-RO hybrid water system at a remote High Arctic climate research station serving 25 permanent scientists. The site sits 2,800 km from the nearest municipal water grid, accessible only by Twin Otter aircraft for 4 months of the year. Air temperatures routinely reach −45°C, with multi-week stretches of darkness and unpredictable solar irradiance.

This case study documents the engineering decisions, root-cause analysis, performance data, and grant-funded financial model behind a system that achieved 96% uptime over 14 months, produced 11,400 L/day average of WHO-grade drinking water, and operated 100% off-grid via a wind + solar + thermal-recovery hybrid power loop.

01Background & Mission Context

High Arctic research stations sit at the front line of climate science. Stations like the Polar Environment Atmospheric Research Laboratory (PEARL) at Eureka, the Resolute Bay observatory, and Cambridge Bay's Canadian High Arctic Research Station (CHARS) generate critical data on ozone chemistry, permafrost dynamics, and Arctic sea-ice. Yet the people producing this science routinely face one of the most basic operational risks in remote engineering: reliable potable water.

Conventional water for research stations comes from three options, each with serious limitations:

  • Trucked or flown-in bottled water: $0.42–$0.86 per litre delivered; weather-dependent; produces 1.2 tonnes of plastic waste per year for a 25-person station.
  • Local snowmelt or lake withdrawal: Requires manual labour; lake ice is up to 2.4 m thick by March; surface microbial contamination concerns; sediment and TOC1 spikes during freshet.
  • Centralized municipal hookup: Does not exist; nearest treatment plant is 2,800 km away.

The research community has consistently flagged water as the single most fragile resource at remote sites. A 2023 survey of 14 Canadian Arctic research stations2 reported that 71% had experienced unscheduled water outages of 48+ hours within the previous 24 months, and 94% relied on diesel-generator-pumped supply chains incompatible with net-zero research mandates.

"Our entire ozone-depletion monitoring schedule shifted twice in 2023 because the station's water reserves dropped below the emergency floor. We needed a system that doesn't care if the plane shows up." — Senior atmospheric scientist, Polar Continental Shelf Program

02The Challenge — Root Cause Analysis

Before selecting equipment, Aarvish engineers ran a structured Ishikawa (fishbone) analysis to map every potential failure mode and chase each one to a root cause. Conventional decentralized water systems fail in Arctic conditions for reasons that span six independent categories. Addressing all of them together is what separates a system that survives 14 months from one that fails in 14 days.

03Technical Solution — Architecture & Components

The deployed system is an Arctic-hardened variant of Aarvish's standard AWG-RO hybrid platform. Three engineering deltas separate it from a temperate-climate unit:

3.1 Thermal Envelope & Process Sequence

The full container is wrapped in a 200mm vacuum-insulated panel (VIP) shell with R-50 effective insulation — roughly 6x the thermal performance of conventional polyurethane. Inside that shell, every fluid path is heat-traced to maintain a minimum 4°C process-water temperature even when ambient sits at −45°C.

Figure 2 — Process Flow Diagram: Arctic AWG-RO Water Production

End-to-end water path showing thermal management at each stage. Heat-traced segments shown in orange.

Figure 2. Six-stage Arctic AWG-RO process flow. Boron-doped polyamide RO membranes maintain 99.6% TDS rejection at 4°C feedwater — conventional PA membranes drop to 89% below 8°C.

3.2 Power Architecture: Wind + Solar + Thermal-Store

Off-grid Arctic operation cannot rely on solar alone. From mid-November through early February, the station experiences 88 days of continuous darkness. The power solution is a triple-source hybrid:

  • Vertical-axis wind turbine (12 kW peak): Cold-rated to −55°C with cobalt-magnet generator and lithium-iron-phosphate (LiFePO₄) buffering. Average winter wind at Resolute Bay: 7.2 m/s.
  • Bifacial PV array (8 kWp): Generates from 18% albedo snow-reflected light even in marginal light conditions. Active in 8 of 12 months.
  • Thermal energy store (90 kWh-thermal): Phase-change paraffin block stores excess wind energy as heat, releasing it slowly to maintain process temperatures during multi-day calm periods.

7.1 Scientific Output Impact

With water no longer a scheduling bottleneck, the host research station logged its highest-ever measurement-hour count for ozone profiling and ice-core preservation in 2025. Two additional Visiting Scientist slots were opened in the 2025 field season — a direct consequence of removing the per-person water-cost ceiling.

What This Means for the Arctic Research Network

This deployment proved that decentralized, fully autonomous water production at 74°N is not an engineering aspiration — it is a deployed reality. Aarvish has since received Letters of Intent from four additional Canadian Arctic research stations (Eureka, Alert, Cambridge Bay, Cape Bounty) for replication during the 2026–2027 field seasons.

08Lessons Learned & Forward Plan

Three findings will reshape Aarvish's next Arctic deployments:

  • Thermal-store overdimensioning pays back. The 90 kWh-thermal PCM block was 30% larger than initially scoped. That headroom absorbed every multi-day calm event without falling back to diesel. We recommend +25% on future Arctic units.
  • Operator handover materials matter more than equipment specs. The system spent 18% of its troubleshooting hours on operator-procedural questions, not actual hardware faults. Aarvish has now formalized a 60-page station-handbook and quarterly video review with the operator.
  • Bandwidth-limited telemetry forces priorities. The Iridium uplink permits only ~1 KB every 15 minutes. We rebuilt the telemetry schema to prioritize "system health byte" first, with detailed logs queued for daily burst transmission.
